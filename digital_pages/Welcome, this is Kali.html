The Challenge

Kali is a speculative design project that imagines a dystopian near-future through the interface of a retro-styled computer operating system, where users navigate popup windows and icons, earn tokens through a computer-vision rock-paper-scissors game, and spend them to unlock content like news, music, and ad-blockers. The familiar retro aesthetic makes the underlying picture more jarring: a future where access to information and basic functionality is monetized and mediated by propaganda, with the commentary on social injustice emerging through exploration rather than being stated outright. The visual designs were created in Canva and exported as PNGs, while the interactive logic, hitbox mapping, state management, and token system were built in p5.js.

The Approach

First, I designed and gain visual direction for the project. The graphics were built in Canva, drawing on Windows 3.1 and other retro computing aesthetics as reference. The choice was deliberate rather than nostalgic. By rendering the future through visual language that already reads as old and outdated, the project creates a kind of hyperreality, imagining how people in the future might look back at the technology of the past and asking what feels familiar, clunky, or quietly sinister about the systems we use now.

I continued by building the mechanics of the project. The second phase combined those static graphics with interaction logic in p5.js, mapping hitboxes onto the imagery and layering in the systems that carry the project's argument. That included a computer-vision rock-paper-scissors game for earning tokens, a paywall gating access to features, intrusive popup ads, and paid unlocks for things that should be ordinary, listening to music, drawing, and reading the news. Each mechanic was chosen to make the commentary something users experience through friction rather than read as a statement.

Lastly, I playtested and continued to refine the project. The final phase involved putting the project in front of human playtesters and watching how they actually moved through it. Observing where people got stuck, what they discovered on their own, and what they missed entirely drove successive rounds of refinement to the interactions, pacing, and discoverability, shaping the project into something that communicated its message through exploration rather than instruction.

The Outcome

The result was a fully working interactive experience that people could actually explore rather than just read about, shown to the class and at the IMA end-of-semester show. Users moved through the retro desktop discovering features on their own, earning tokens through the rock-paper-scissors game, and running into the paywalls, ads, and locked functionality that carried the project's argument, which meant the critique of a monetized, propaganda-mediated future landed through friction and discovery rather than being stated outright. The project also evolved substantially from the original proposal, which began as an audio piece built around radio recordings before pivoting to an interactive game, and it ended up delivering the same message about social injustice more effectively than the original concept would have.
