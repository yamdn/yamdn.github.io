Exoskin

Role Designer || Individual
Tools Rhino, Grasshopper, 3D printings

The Challenge

In this project, the goal was to experiment with Grasshopper, a parametric design tool within Rhino, while creating something decorative and wearable. I decided to design an arm band because wearable objects introduce additional challenges, such as fit, comfort, and flexibility, which are not always easy to account for in digital design. Influenced by the structural logic of exoskeletons, Exoskin was named for its snuggle fit against one part of the user's body. This concept guided both the visual language and the structural form of the arm bands. In the end, I created three different designs with multiple prototypes that were flexible and could comfortably fit around my arm.

The Approach

I began by sketching several ideas for decorative objects, initially exploring forms like a flower vase before shifting toward an arm band. The flowing, repeating patterns reminded me of vines and skeletal structures, which aligned well with the Exoskin concept. Using Grasshopper, I built the arm band form with basic components such as arcs, series, rotations, and pipes, allowing the design to grow and repeat in an organic, bone-like way. After establishing the general shape, I printed three small prototypes to test sizing and fit on my arm.

To support the wearable nature of the piece, I chose TPU filament instead of PLA so the arm bands could bend and stretch when worn. Grasshopper’s parametric interface made it easy to adjust dimensions and thickness based on feedback from the prototypes. After refining the design, I printed three final arm bands that could be worn and accessorized, successfully combining computational design, material exploration, and the exoskeleton-inspired concept behind Exoskin.

The Outcome

Through this project, I gained a much stronger understanding of how Grasshopper functions and how it can be combined with Rhino to create parametric designs. While I produced three final arm bands, the process showed me that there were almost endless possibilities for experimenting with different shapes, sizes, and surface textures by adjusting parameters in Grasshopper. Working with TPU also helped me better understand material choice, especially when designing wearable objects that need flexibility and comfort. Overall, the project encouraged me to think more iteratively and explore how computational design can quickly adapt to both aesthetic and functional needs.
