The Challenge

One of the primary symptoms of Parkinson’s disease is tremors in a dominant arm. Tremors are defined as rhythmic oscillations in the frequency range of 3-5Hz. The treatment for these tremors is typically a dopamine boost, which transitions the patient from the “Off” state to the “On” state. However, if too much dopamine is provided, an additional symptom call dyskinesia occurs. This is characterized by rhythmic dance-like movements in the frequency range of 5-7Hz. It is essential to have this information to properly medicate a patient so that the patient is “On”, but not too “On”.

The Approach

To implement this diagnostic tool, I focused on real-time frequency analysis of accelerometer data to distinguish between different motor conditions. The process begins with high-speed sampling of the Circuit Playground’s onboard accelerometer. By calculating the 3D vector magnitude √(x2 + y2 + z2), I’m able to capture the total intensity of a tremor regardless of the device's orientation. I then pass this raw signal through a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) pipeline. To ensure data integrity, I utilized FFT.dcRemoval() to eliminate the gravity constant and applied a Hamming window to minimize spectral leakage before computing the magnitudes across the frequency spectrum.

Once I had the frequency and magnitude, I used simple threshold checks to classify the motion into one of three states. Based on the detected condition, I updated both the onboard NeoPixels and an LCD screen to give immediate visual feedback. A button toggle controlled whether the system was active, making it easy to start and stop detection without resetting the board. Overall, the flow was: collect motion data → run FFT → detect the dominant movement pattern → display the result, keeping everything responsive and easy to interpret in real time.

The Outcome

The device successfully detected and classified Parkinson’s-related movement patterns in real time using motion data analysis. It provided immediate visual feedback through onboard LEDs and an LCD screen, making the results easy to understand at a glance. This demonstrated that wearable sensor data can be used effectively to recognize and communicate different movement states in a simple and accessible way.
